**Basic Statement Sentences**
The first thing we're gonna be covering tonight is basic Japanese statement sentences. Basic Japanese statement sentences are **X wa Y desu**. So it's a little bit of a formula, but I'm gonna explain how that goes; don't worry, it should be easy.

So an example of this would be: **Boku wa Andy desu**. **Boku** is a masculine version of the word "I." The normal version would be **watashi**; a more masculine version would be **ore**. But anyway, **Boku wa Andy desu**, and of course, I'm Andy. So let's see how this whole thing works together and take a little bit closer of a look at this formula.

**X** in this formula is the topic; it's the thing we're talking about in this sentence. **Wa** is a Japanese particle. There are many particles in Japanese, and they generally mark something before them; they describe something about it. In this case, the **wa** particle marks whatever the topic is in a sentence. So in this case, it marks X. **Y** is something about X; it's what X is. And **desu** is the phrase basically meaning "it is."

So in the example sentence before, we had **Boku wa Andy desu**. **Boku** is the topic, **wa** marks **boku**, **Andy** is something about **boku** (it's about me, so obviously it's my name Andy), and **desu** is "it is." My name is Andy. Once again, I want to mention that you don't need **boku wa** or **watashi wa** when you're introducing yourself. You could just say, "**Andy desu.**" Lots of Japanese people just say it that way; it's perfectly okay. So if you can't get **boku wa** or **watashi wa** off the tip of your tongue, it's perfectly okay. Just the same, you say your name and **desu**. So don't worry about that too much.

Let's go ahead into some more sentences now. Our example sentences for **X wa Y desu** are the following:

**Banana wa amai desu.** Bananas are sweet. Now this is interesting because Japanese people tend to refer to bananas as sweet, and I never really thought of them as sweet. I mean, they're not *not* sweet, but they've got this sort of unique taste that I've never thought of as sweet. But in Japanese, we refer to bananas as **amai**. So **amai** means sweet; it's an adjective which means sweet. So we say **Banana wa amai desu**. **Banana** is the topic; it's marked by **wa**, the topic marker. Then we have something about bananas: **amai**, they're sweet, and **desu**. So this can be "it is" or "they are" if it's a plural type of thing, right? There's no plurals in Japanese, so it's just picked up by context that we're talking about bananas in general. If I was just holding one banana and I was saying "**Banana wa amai desu**," it would be obvious that I was just talking about that one banana.

Our next sentence is **Kyō wa nichiyōbi desu**. So, today is Sunday. **Kyō** is today. **Questions in Japanese: か (ka) particle**
Alright, so our next section, Section 2 of Genki Lesson 1, is how to ask questions in Japanese. It's pretty easy; all you have to do to ask a question in polite Japanese is add **ka**. Now this is another marker, yet another particle just like **wa**, but this is called the question marker, and we add it to the end of a polite sentence. So like this: we have a polite statement, for example, **X wa Y desu**, plus **ka**.

So let's go ahead and use our previous sentences from the last section and just add **ka** and see what happens. Now you'll notice here that I did not add question marks; mostly it would just be **ka** expressing that it's a question. So let's go ahead and just read through these sentences:

**Banana wa amai desu ka?** Are bananas sweet? Or if there's just one banana someone else is holding, you might say the same exact thing and just mean "is that banana sweet?" So if we go back to our **X wa Y desu** polite statement sentence, we have **banana** as **X** (the topic), **wa** marks the topic, **amai** is about the topic (sweet), **desu** is "it is," and **ka** is the question. Basically, think of it that way: we're speaking the question mark in polite Japanese only. If I was speaking informally just with Yuki or something, I might just say "**Banana wa amai?**" You drop the **desu** there as well, but "**Banana wa amai?**" just with rising intonation, just like in English basically. **Using の (no) to add Information**
Let's go ahead and jump into the final section of this lesson, which is using **no** to add information in Japanese. Alright, boom! We can give more information about a noun using the **no** particle. So we've learned three particles today, right? We've learned the **wa** particle which marks a topic, we've learned the **ka** particle which goes at the end of the sentence and marks the whole sentence as a question, and now we're about to learn the **no** particle, which basically gives more information about another noun.

So this is how it works: first we have **Noun A**, the first noun, followed by **no**, and then **Noun B**. Now, the second noun, **Noun B**, is actually the main item in the sentence. **Noun A** is any added information or anything we want to say extra about **B**, right? So that's the formula; it's a little weird looking at it right now. I'll explain it with sentences in a few minutes.

But it can also be used as the possessive. The exact same formula, **Noun A no Noun B**, can be the possessive. So basically apostrophe-S in English, right? But in this case, **Noun A** is the owner, then we have **no**, and **Noun B** is the item that the person owns. Let's go ahead and jump into some examples so that you can get a better idea of how this looks.

The first one is **Andy no keitai**. **Andy no keitai**. **Keitai** is a cell phone. Basically, in modern Japanese, most people don't say **keitai** really; they usually say **sumaho**, which is katakana English for smartphone, but I still use **keitai**. Anyway, **Andy no keitai**. This is the possessive, so it's "Andy's cell phone." Or **boku no keitai**, "my cell phone." So in this case, it's the possessive, right?

But in our next sentence: **yōchien no sensei**. **Yōchien** is kindergarten. **Sensei** is, of course—I'm sure you know, maybe not, but you probably know if you're at this point—teacher. So, "kindergarten teacher." Now this is not the possessive; it's similar, right, but it's not the possessive like we think of it in English. It's basically describing more about **Noun B**. **Noun B** in this situation is **sensei**, and what kind of teacher? Let's talk about it more: well, they're a kindergarten teacher. So we say **yōchien no sensei**.

In our next sentence, we have **yakyū no senshu**. Baseball player. So the person is a player of some sport, right? They're a player—**senshu** is a player. What kind of player? Well, they're a baseball player. **Yakyū no senshu**. So if you look at these sentences, it's kind of the same order as in English, except there's an extra step, right? In English we have "kindergarten teacher," there's nothing in between. We have "baseball player," nothing in between.

So here are some sentences using **no**, and we're gonna use the **X wa Y desu** formula to sort of show you how this would work in an actual sentence. What's great about the **no** particle is that we can take simple sentences like before and we can sort of make them more complicated, which is a good thing; we can talk about more.

Our first sentence is: **Yakyū no senshu wa tsuyoi desu.** Baseball players are strong. So our **X** is **yakyū no senshu**, that's the topic. **Wa tsuyoi desu**. Baseball players are strong. **Tsuyoi** means strong.

Our next sentence is: **Andy no keitai wa kuroi desu.** My cell phone—Andy's cell phone—is black. **Andy no keitai wa kuroi desu**.

The next sentence is a little bit more difficult. You'll notice that we use **no** twice, which is what's great: we can have a topic that uses **no** and talks more about something, and the description can also use **no**. In this case, we have: **Watashi no okāsan wa yōchien no sensei desu.** My mom is a kindergarten teacher. **Watashi no okāsan** or **boku no okāsan**—my mom. So in this case, it's the possessive, "my mom" or "my mother." And she's the topic. What are we gonna talk about? My mother. What are we gonna say about my mother? Well, she's a **yōchien no sensei desu**. She is a kindergarten teacher. And that's true, actually; my mother is a kindergarten teacher. So, **Boku no okāsan wa yōchien no sensei desu**, right? So we use **no** in both parts of the sentence, in the X and the Y.
