**これ, それ, etc. (Kore, Sore, Are, Dore)**

Please see this section. **Kore** is something near me (the speaker).

**Sore** is something near you (the listener).

**Are** is for things far away from both people.

The last one is **Dore**, which means "which" of three or more things.

And then... the four points that you need to know when you use them in conversation patterns.

That's all for using these very simple things: **Kore, Sore, Are, and Dore**.

**Kore** (this), **Sore** (that), **Are** (that over there), **Dore** (which).

We use them to say things like: "**Kore wa nan desu ka?**" (What is this?)

"What is this?" is a common sentence that you need to use all the time to speak.

For example, if you point at a door and ask, "**Kore wa nan desu ka?**" (What is this?) 

Or if you see Japanese paper—basically paper—and ask: "**Kore wa nan desu ka?**" (What is this?)

The answer is: "**Pikachuu desu.**" (It is Pikachu.)

In Japanese, this expression means Pikachu. "**Pika-pika**" is the onomatopoeia for the sound of electric sparks. I'm a big fan of Pikachu; it's a Pokemon video after all!

Next sentence: "**Are wa nan desu ka?**" (What is that over there?)

Like if you see an **AirPods** case... "**Are wa nan desu ka?**"

And then **Dore**: "**Dore desu ka?**" (Which one is it?) when you aren't sure which one.

Let's explain the meanings with a conversation:

"**Kore wa oishii desu ka?**" (Is this delicious?)
"**Nan desu ka, sore?**" (What is that?) 
*Notice: "Sore" was at the end. This is another way to say "**Sore wa nan desu ka?**" In conversation, you can mix up word order. Japanese people say "**Nan desu ka, sore?**" all the time.*
"**Kore wa natto desu yo.**" (This is Natto, you know.)
"**Uwa, hen na nioi.**" (Ugh, a strange smell.)
"**Shiranai.**" (I don't know/I've never seen it.)—which is the informal version.

**この, その, etc. (Kono, Sono, Ano, Dono)**

Now, Japanese also uses **Kono, Sono, Ano, and Dono**.

There is a specific rule: you use these when there is a **noun** immediately following.
**Kono** [noun] (this...), **Sono** [noun] (that...), **Ano** [noun] (that... over there), and **Dono** [noun] (which...).

So, the difference between **Kore** and **Kono** is that **Kono** must bind to a noun. Let’s study some simple sentences:

"**Kono hon**" (this book), "**Sono hon**" (that book), "**Ano hon**" (that book over there), "**Dono hon**" (which book).

"**Kono hon wa oishii desu ka?**" (Wait—"Is this book delicious?" No, I meant...) 
"**Kono hon wa omoshiroi desu ka?**" (Is this book interesting?)
**Omoshiroi** means interesting or funny depending on the context.

"**Dono hon wa omoshiroi desu ka?**" (Which book is interesting?)

"Is that Pokemon Pikachu?" 
In Japanese: "**Sono Pokemon wa Pikachuu desu ka?**"
"**Ano Pokemon wa Pikachuu desu ka?**" (Is that Pokemon over there Pikachu?)

Next example:
"**Kono hon wa ikura desu ka?**" (How much is this book?)
"**Dono hon desu ka?**" (Which book?) 
"**Kono hon desu.**" (This book.)
"**Ah, sono hon desu ne. Sen-en desu.**" (Ah, that book. It's 1000 yen.)
"**Arigatou gozaimasu.**" (Thank you very much.)

**Ikura** means "how much." When you want to buy something, ask: "**Ikura desu ka?**"

**ここ, そこ, etc. (Koko, Soko, Asoko, Doko)**

The next section is for locations: here, there, over there, and where.

**Koko** means "here" (near the speaker).
**Soko** means "there" (near the listener).
**Asoko** means "over there" (far from both).
**Doko** means "where."

A super important question: "**Toire wa doko desu ka?**" (Where is the restroom?)
The answer might be: "**Koko desu**" (It's here), "**Soko desu**" (It's there), or "**Asoko desu**" (It's over there).

Actual sentences:
"**Koko wa doko desu ka?**" (Where am I? / Where is this place?)
"**Soko janai desu. Asoko desu.**" (It's not there. It's over there.) 
"**Janai**" means "is not."

Let's look at a store conversation:
"**Sumimasen.**" (Excuse me.)
"**Hai, irasshaimase.**" (Yes, welcome.)
"**Etto... Yotsubato wa doko desu ka?**" (Uh... where is Yotsubato?)
*Note: "**Etto**" is an **interjection** like "uh" or "um." Using it makes you sound very natural.*
*Note: "**Yotsubato!**" is the name of a famous manga.*
"**Yotsubato desu ka? Asoko desu.**" (Yotsubato? It's over there.)
"**Koko desu ka?**" (Is it here?)
"**Ee, soko desu.**" (Yes, right there.)

**だれの (Dare no)**

Japanese uses "**Dare no**" for "whose." 
The formula is: **Dare no + Noun**.
**Dare** means "who." The "**no**" particle indicates possession, so "**Dare no**" becomes "whose."

"**Dare no kaban desu ka?**" (Whose bag is it?)
"**Kore wa dare no hon desu ka?**" (Whose book is this?)

Example conversation:
"**Ano, sumimasen.**" (Um, excuse me.)
"**Kore wa okyakusama no saifu desu ka?**" (Is this the customer's wallet?)
*Note: You call a customer "**Okyakusama**" in Japanese.*
"**Iie, watashi no saifu janai desu.**" (No, it's not my wallet.)
"**Kore wa dare no saifu desu ka?**" (Whose wallet is this?)

**noun+も (Mo)**

The particle "**Mo**" is super important. It means "also" or "too."

"**Kare wa amerikajin desu.**" (He is American.)
"**Boku mo amerikajin desu.**" (I am also American.)

"**Banana wa amai desu.**" (Bananas are sweet.)
"**Ringo mo amai desu.**" (Apples are also sweet.)

For fans of *Animal Crossing* (**Atsumare Doubutsu no Mori**):
"**Doubutsu no Mori wa tanoshii desu yo.**" (Animal Crossing is fun, you know.)
"**Baiohazaado mo tanoshii desu.**" (Resident Evil is also fun.)
*Note: Resident Evil is called "**Biohazard**" in Japan.*

Another example:
"**Kore wa dare no kaban desu ka?**" (Whose bag is this?)
"**C-san no desu. Kore mo.**" (It's C-san's. This one too.)
"**Sono kaban wa wakarimasen.**" (I don't know about that bag.)
*Note: "**Wakarimasen**" is the polite way to say "I don't know" or "I don't understand."*

**noun+じゃない (Janai)**

To make a negative statement: "**X wa Y janai desu**" (X is not Y).

"**Banana wa aka janai desu.**" (Bananas are not red.)
"**Kyou wa getsuyoubi janai desu.**" (Today is not Monday.)

Example:
"**Paatii wa ashita desu ka?**" (Is the party tomorrow?)
"**Ashita janai desu. Doyoubi desu yo.**" (It's not tomorrow. It's Saturday!)
"**Ando-san no paatii wa itsu desu ka?**" (When is Ando-san's party?)
"**Ando-san no paatii janai desu. E?**" (It's not Ando-san's party. What?)
"**Sou desu ka? Un, boku no paatii desu yo.**" (Is that so? Yeah, it's my party!)

**ね (Ne) and よ (Yo) particles**

"**Ne**" is like saying "...right?" or "...isn't it?" It is used for confirmation.
"**Yo**" is used to provide new information, like saying "...you know!"

"**Banana wa amai desu yo.**" (Bananas are sweet, you know!)
"**Banana wa amai desu ne.**" (Bananas are sweet, aren't they?)

Example:
"**B-san no paatii desu ne.**" (It's B-san's party, right?)
"**Un, boku no paatii desu yo.**" (Yeah, it's my party!)
"**Fuun, sou desu ka. Tanoshii desu yo.**" (I see. It's fun, you know!)
"**Uso!**" (No way! / You're lying!)
